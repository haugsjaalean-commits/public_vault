
## The philosophy of organization

This is hands down the most important section in the tutorial. The way you choose to organize your vault will decide everything. Given this, the stage where we try to decide how to organize our vault is often very overwhelming (at least for me), and it's very easy to get distracted by things that don't matter, or simply never start because you're always trying to decide what the best way to move forwards is; in fact, in [[The Thinking Person's Set Up (FULL SET UP)]], Vicky Zhao says that only 1% of our time in Obsidian should be spent thinking about how to go about organization. I think that, for someone like me, this number is too small and would only hurt my process; however, I do think that it's important to embrasse a certain *Tao of organization*, where we realize that we can neither spend all of our time thinking about organizing, nor none of our time thinking about it. I believe it is by proceeding like this that we will get the most value out of a tool like Obsidian, as imperfect as it may be.

### The simple way I've been thinking about organization in Obsidian

Assuming no prior knowledge of my theories, I will try to explain in as succinct a manner possible my philosophy when it comes to organizing notes in Obsidian. 
#### My idea for how to achieve organization in Obsidian

In Obsidian, we want to organize **notes**. I believe that, if we want to organize these notes, we have to think of each note as being an *object*. Each object has its own characteristics which describe its nature. These "characteristics" about are purely theoretical and exist only in the mind of the organizer. What I mean by this is that, when we create a note, we already have an idea about the nature of the object that it describes. If my note is something like `dog.md`, then I already know that it should be a child of `mammals.md`. The trick is figuring out a way to model our mental connexions into real data types. This is exactly what I explain below. However, once we have successfully decided on the way in which we want to represent our objects, we need to place them in our vault.

I think that the best method for organizing objects in Obsidian follows a tree-like structure. In this tree like structure, we simply create what I'm going to call ***search pathways***. Each search pathways begins at the origine note, which I usual give the name of "MOC". A search pathway is simply a series of objects of smaller and smaller generalities, which continues until the desired note (object) finds its place in the structure. It's true, of course, that multiple search pathways have the potential of leading to the same note, and this is perfectly fine; in fact, it fits perfectly with my [[semi-rigid organization]] model. 

##### Two different ways of positioning objects in the structure

If we want to position an object in our MOC, we have multiple choices. We can either use links, or we can use [[metadata]]. When we use links, objects are located in the contents of our `.md` files; however, when we use metadata, objects are located in our `.base` files. 

N.B. Both methods can be used in tandem. 


#### My idea for how to model the different **datatypes** and **semi-objects** at play in the organization of notes

This is the toughest part of organization--the true crux of organization. 

As stated above, we want to figure out the simplest and most direct way of representing the complexe mental relationships that exist between objects with this real world tool called Obsidian; this is no easy task indeed. This is the type of thing that one can spend a life time perfecting, and I plan very much on doing so. 

##### Describing the differing types of objects 

Although not necessary, I find that one of the most efficient ways of organizing information involves describing different types (or classes) of objects, which can be reused over and over again. 




My first recommendation is the use of [[tags]] for describing the different types of objects.  





I think that the most effective way of defining the individual characteristics of objects is by thinking of each note as belonging to a class. I also think that it makes the most sense for each note to only ever have one class, because we can think of each class type as a compressed search pathway representing the lineage of the class.
