**The principles of organization** are the building blocks of the entire **structure of information** that is **discrete organization**. We have knowledge, and we want to communicate it. There are a theoretically infinite number of ways of doing this. We could simply give a big list of all of our items and their characteristics. This would technically be achieving the goal of communicating knowledge, but it certainly wouldn't satisfy the part of our brains which needs things to make sense. That is where organization comes in; indeed, just like there are multiple ways of creating a map (satellite view, topographic map, color coded, etc.), there are multiple ways of presenting information, which will have different uses for the human mind. This is why I called this note "organization philosophy" in a previous version of the theory of information - we are deciding what is important to us as humans when it comes to displaying information, and that is a purely philosophical debate with no clear-cut answer.

# Philosophy of organization

I believe that organization is the most direct and clearest way of representing knowledge.

> [!info]+ Definition of **true organization**
> **True organization** is the most direct way of describing objects and their relationships to each other (this is knowledge).

## What is organization

I believe that the following three steps (in order) are the fundamental building blocks of what it means to organize:
1. **Grouping**: Items are continuously funneled into narrower and narrower groups.
2. **Sorting** (triage): Items contained in a given point are sorted.
3. **Displaying**: Items (and any of their characteristics) are displayed based on the parameters.

Think of the 3 step process like this:
1. We need to put the items into the category they belong in.
2. We need to sort them within this category based on their form or color, etc.
3. We need to decide which way to turn and twist them for display.

N.B. I think that the third step is potentially not part of true organization, mais c'est à voir.

# Rigidity of organization

As mentioned in the parent file, the rigidity of organization can change how the KG is created. I believe that there are exactly 3 types of rigidity:
1. [[loose organization]]
2. [[semi-rigid organization]]
3. [[rigid organization]]

## Tableau récapitulatif

| rigidity of organization | KIs can exist in multiple branches | KIs can exist multiple times in the same branch |
| ------------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| loose                    | True                               | True                                            |
| semi-rigid               | True                               | False                                           |
| rigid                    | False                              | False                                           |

## Philosophical implications
The debate is which level of rigidity is to be considered as *true organization*. I would argue that [[rigid organization]] is the only true type of organization, as all others break the rules of reality; however I think that there are very strong arguments for [[semi-rigid organization]] as well, as it is more aligned with a more theoretical conception of organization, where we simply choose filters and see results.

## Implications of [[multi-level organization]]

I theorize that, by using multi-level organization with the more rigid methods of organization, we can achieve the same effect as looser ones.

You might question the utility of going through all of this work just to achieve the same thing that we could've achieved with a looser method of organization. This is a fair question to raise, but it's important to understand that this whole theory of information was never intended to be something fully put into real world practice. Instead, for me, this theory represents a tool we can use to inspect our personal use of organization by comparing it to the theoretical model. This brings me to the answer of the question: using multi level organization gives us a clear separation between different levels of organization. This means that we can clearly see when we are organizing KUs vs when we are organizing organization itself. To me, this is the very purpose of the theory: give us insights into the inner workings of organization and help us conceptualize more powerful or specialized knowledge systems.

### Examples

#### Fruit example

We have a list of fruit. Each fruit has a native country and a list of potential colors. We want to first group the fruit based on their native country, and then, within these groups, group the fruit based on color. The only problem is that the fruit can have multiple colors, which means that this cannot work in rigid organization; it could, however, work in semi-rigid organization. This is nice, but what if we wanted to try to model this situation in rigid organization? We would need to use [[multi-level organization]]. We need to create multiple duplications of the starting set of KU (the fruit). Then we need to organize all of these by country and, for each color, make another organization for colors. We need to do this process of duplications as many times as there are colors of fruit. The fruit example canvas shows the full process.
